Excellent clarification! This is a crucial decision point in the design, and it fundamentally changes the feel of the game. You've basically identified the two major paths we can take:
 * Individual Theaters: A "tableau-building" game where each player creates their own scoring puzzle.
 * A Shared Theater: An "area control" game where all players compete over a central, shared space.
Let's break down your idea for individual theaters first, and then I'll answer your question about tracking points in a shared theater, as understanding both will help us decide which path is best.
Path 1: Individual Player Theaters (Your Idea)
This is a very popular and successful style of game! Each player gets their own small theater board (say, a 4x5 grid, giving them 20 seats to fill over the game).
How it Works: On your turn, you draw a card from the central deck and place it into a seat in your own theater grid. The game ends when the deck runs out. Everyone then calculates the total points from their personal board.
Pros of this approach:
 * Clear Ownership: There is zero confusion about points. Everything on your board is yours. Final scoring is simple and clean.
 * Focus on the Puzzle: The game becomes a fascinating personal puzzle. You're trying to optimize your own little engine without others directly messing with your placements.
 * Reduced "Analysis Paralysis": Turns can be faster because you are primarily concerned with your own layout, not a constantly changing central board.
 * Feels Constructive: Players feel a strong sense of accomplishment from building their perfect theater.
This directly addresses your concern about the "take that" mechanic. But it also creates a new design challenge.
Handling "Take That" in an Individual Theater
You're right, playing a Tall Person or Noisy Patron into your own theater feels terrible. It's like being forced to harm yourself. These cards become "duds" that you hate drawing. Here is the elegant solution that honors your idea of an "optional add-on":
The "Gift" Rule: When you play a card with a negative effect (like Tall Person or Noisy Patron), you have a choice:
 * Place it in your own theater (and suffer the consequences).
 * Place it in an available seat in an opponent's theater.
This is brilliant because it turns a "dud" card into one of the most interesting decisions in the game! Do you place the Noisy Patron in your rival's high-scoring VIP section? Or do you place it in your own theater in a harmless corner to avoid making an enemy? It creates a tense, semi-aggressive interaction that is driven by specific cards, rather than being the default for the whole game.
Path 2: Shared Theater & How to Track Points
Now, to answer your excellent question: If we all play on one board, how do we track points?
This is solved with a simple, elegant component: Player Markers.
Imagine that in addition to the deck of cards, each of the 4 players gets a set of 15 small tokens in their color (e.g., Red, Blue, Green, Yellow). We can call them "Usher's Marks" or "Seat Claims."
How it Works:
 * The large theater layout is in the center of the table.
 * On your turn, you draw a patron card and place it on any empty seat in the shared theater.
 * You then place one of your colored Player Markers directly on top of that card.
 * The game ends when the deck is empty. Now it's time for final scoring.
Final Scoring in a Shared Theater:
You go through the theater row by row. When you get to a patron card, you look at the colored marker on it to see who "owns" it. Then you calculate the score for that specific patron based on its surroundings.
 * Example: I (the Blue player) placed a VIP in the front row. I put my blue marker on it. Later, you (the Red player) place a Kid right next to my VIP. At the end of the game, when we score my VIP, it gets its base points and front-row bonus, but it will also get a negative point penalty because it's adjacent to a Kid, even though you played that Kid. The final score for that VIP card goes to me.
The "Take That" Feels Different Here:
In this shared model, playing a Tall Person feels less like a direct personal attack and more like strategic area denial. You're not saying "I'm giving you negative points." You're saying, "I am making this seat a bad spot for anyone to use." It becomes a tactical move to control the board, which can feel more strategic and less mean.

Philosophy: Hindrance, Not Harm
This is a great design principle. We don't want a player to feel like their entire game was ruined by one or two lucky plays from an opponent. The goal should be to force opponents into making suboptimal plays or to create an interesting new puzzle for them to solve.
Based on this, let's refine our "Giftable" cards. These are the cards that, when you play them, you can choose to place in an opponent's theater instead of your own.
 * The Tall Person: This card is already a perfect hindrance. Giving one patron -2 VP is not game-ending, but it's certainly not ideal. It creates a "bad spot" on the recipient's board that they now have to strategically place a less-valuable patron behind. No changes needed.
 * The Noisy Patron: As designed, this card gives -1 VP to all orthogonally adjacent patrons. If placed in a dense cluster, this could be -3 or -4 VP, which might tip from "hindrance" into "harmful." Let's tune it slightly to give the recipient more control.
   * Refined Rule: A Noisy Patron gives any orthogonally adjacent Standard Patron card -2 VP. Other special types (VIPs, Teachers, etc.) are too absorbed in their own worlds to be bothered.
   * Why it works: This makes the Noisy Patron a surgical tool. It primarily devalues the most common "filler" cards, forcing the opponent to use their more valuable special patrons to surround it, which might not be their ideal placement.
 * The Obstructed View Seat (New Idea): This is a new type of "Gift" card that is a pure hindrance with zero negative points. It's not a person, but a feature of the seat itself.
   * Card Type: A Blocker/Condition.
   * Rule: When you play this card, place it in any empty seat. The Obstructed View Seat is worth 0 VP. Furthermore, patrons sitting directly adjacent (orthogonally) to this seat cannot score any of their special bonuses.
   * Example: A Bespectacled Patron next to it wouldn't get their front-row bonus. A Critic next to it wouldn't get their aisle bonus. Two Lovebirds separated by it couldn't form a scoring pair.
   * Why it works: This is the ultimate hindrance. It's a dead spot that brilliantly disrupts an opponent's plan without directly subtracting from their score. It's all about denying potential.
This gives us a fantastic trio of "Gift" cards that all feel different: one subtracts points (Tall Person), one neutralizes other cards (Noisy Patron), and one blocks potential (Obstructed View Seat).
Mechanic: Preventing Player Targeting
This is a critical rule for maintaining a friendly table. The goal is to "spread the love" of these hindrance cards. Your instinct is spot on. Here is a clean, thematic way to implement this:
The "Manager's Attention" Token
We'll add one special token to the game's components called the "Manager's Attention" token.
 * The Rule: When you "Gift" a card to an opponent, you take the "Manager's Attention" token and place it on that opponent's player board.
 * The Restriction: No player can "Gift" a card to the player who currently has the "Manager's Attention" token.
 * How it Moves: The token only moves when someone else "Gifts" a card to a different, eligible opponent.
Let's walk through an example in a 4-player game (You, Bob, Carol, David):
 * On your turn, you draw a Tall Person and decide to gift it to Bob. You place the card in Bob's theater and place the "Manager's Attention" token on Bob's board.
 * On Carol's turn, she draws a Noisy Patron. She cannot gift it to Bob because he has the token. She must either place it in her own theater or gift it to you or David. She chooses to gift it to David. She moves the token from Bob's board to David's.
 * Now David is protected, and everyone else is an eligible target again.
This single token elegantly prevents direct, repeated targeting and kingmaking. It forces players to vary their targets, making the interactions feel more dynamic and less personal.

You have absolutely hit on the engine that will give this game incredible legs. A modular system of Layouts + Plays is the perfect way to generate immense replayability. The idea of making them interact in a unique way for each game is the kind of design elegance that players love.
Let's build this system. I envision a two-component setup that combines to create a unique puzzle every time.
Part 1: The Theater Layouts (The "Where")
These are the physical player boards. We'll design a set of them, each with a different structure and strategic feel. For a base game, we could include 4 unique layouts. They are double-sided for even more variety!
Each board is a grid (e.g., 4x5), but the features change:
Layout A: "The Grand Empress"
Structure: A classic, wide theater. Two side aisles, no center aisle.
Strategic Feel: Plenty of aisle seats makes The Critic a strong card. The width makes grouping Kids horizontally very effective.
Layout B: "The Blackbox"
Structure: A deep, narrow theater with only one center aisle. The back "row" is only two seats.
Strategic Feel: Aisle seats are rare and highly contested. Scoring Lovebirds in the tiny back row is a challenge. The long, narrow shape makes the Tall Person's line-of-sight effect much more impactful.
Layout C: "The Royal Theatre"
Structure: Standard shape, but it features two "Royal Box" seats. These seats are off to the side, and for scoring purposes, they count as being both an aisle seat and in the front row simultaneously.
Strategic Feel: The Royal Boxes are the most valuable seats on the board. They create a huge point of contention and a prime spot for a VIP.
Part 2: The Play Cards (The "What")
These cards dictate the unique rules for the game. At the start of the game, you'll draw one random Play card and it will apply to all players. They fall into two categories: Rule Changes and Board Modifications.
This is where your "clever mechanism" comes in. Many Play cards will have a "Setup" instruction that physically alters the chosen Layout board before the game begins.
Here are some examples of Play cards:
Play Card 1: "Opening Night Gala"
Type: Rule Change
Effect: "All VIP patrons are worth an additional +3 VP. All Standard Patrons are now considered Bespectacled Patrons and score accordingly."
This card has no Setup instruction. It's a pure rule change.
Play Card 2: "Fire Safety Inspection"
Type: Board Modification
Setup: "Take 4 'Velvet Rope' tokens and place one on each aisle seat of your theater. These seats cannot be used."
Effect: "At the start of the final round, all players may remove the 'Velvet Rope' tokens from their board."
This card completely changes the value of The Critic and makes the endgame a frantic rush for the newly opened seats.
Play Card 3: "Sold-Out Show"
Type: Rule Change & Board Modification
Setup: "Before the game, take two Standard Patron cards from the box (not the deck) and place them in the two center-most seats of the middle row."
Effect: "Your theater is already filling up! You have two fewer seats to work with."
This makes the board feel tighter and can either help or hinder your starting strategy.
Play Card 4: "Structural Survey"
Type: Board Modification
Setup: "Place two Obstructed View Seat tokens on your board on the seats marked with a small star." (We would print small star icons on specific seats of each layout).
Effect: "These seats were deemed unusable. Nothing can be placed there for the entire game."
This creates permanent dead spots that players must build around.
How Synergy Creates Replayability
Now, imagine the magic of combining these. You never play the same game twice.
Game 1: The Grand Empress + Fire Safety Inspection.
Suddenly, the theater known for its abundance of great aisle seats has no usable aisles for most of the game! The core strength of the layout is nullified, forcing a completely new strategy.
Game 2: The Blackbox + Sold-Out Show.
The already-cramped Blackbox now starts with two seats already filled. The puzzle becomes incredibly tight. The Tall Person becomes even more of a liability in this claustrophobic environment.
Game 3: The Royal Theatre + Structural Survey.
What if the "star" icons for the Structural Survey are right next to the Royal Boxes? Suddenly, the best seats on the board become adjacent to a permanent blocker, making it impossible to score adjacency bonuses there. This masterfully sabotages the board's key feature.
This system does exactly what you want. It's modular, interactive, and inherently expandable. For an expansion, we could simply release a pack of 4 new Layout boards and 8 new Play cards, and the number of unique combinations would grow exponentially.
